As of writing this post, I am a young offensive security professional in the most uncertain years of the tech industry. I recently became a penetration tester (it's been 10 months) and I have loved it since I've joined.

Before this, I had obtained a Bachelor of Engineering and a Bachelor of Computer Science at Curtin University. I really enjoyed my degrees and loved my Uni years, and I'm hoping to do a few posts on some of the projects I did back then. 

After that I got my first Engineering job in WA. You see, I got into Engineering for the reason that most people get into it - curiosity, learning about how things work, designing and seeing your ideas come to life. This job just wasn't what I imagined at all, it was mostly sales and management. I killed it at the job and got paid decently, but I frequently felt like not wanting to go into work the next day. It was getting to me mentally and even interferring with my personal life. What I really wanted was a technically challenging role where I actually got to understand systems fundamentally and that's what I decided to pursue.

I was eager to find a fulfilling job, and got nudged into looking into Cybersecurity by a friend. Being a naive Uni student, I had never really considered that the Cybersecurity industry could offer work that was extremely technically challenging. At this point, all I actually knew about cybersecurity was the CIA triad...

I ended up getting a job as a pentester in offensive security, and it was exactly what I wanted out of a job. Learning about different technologies, and gaining a deep understanding of a system to figure out how it can be exploited - it's a job filled with problem solving and learning.

Do I regret going in to the tech industry because of the uncertainty created due to sophisticated LLMs (or what is branded as AI)? No. I would be lying if I said I didn't think about it, and you always have to take emerging technologies into consideration to be able to adapt. But even if things go sideways, and layoffs happpen - I won't regret taking a risk to do something that I enjoyed and felt fulfilled by in my life. In fact, all that needs to happen for me to stay in the industry is become more of an expert in my field and aim to be at that top-level of skill.
